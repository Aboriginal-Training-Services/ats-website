**How Aboriginal Training Services is helping NAIT students ‚Äî including Indigenous learners ‚Äî gain Transport Canada certification, hands-on drone experience, and skills that connect academic learning with real-world applications.**
<br><br>
Northern Alberta Institute of Technology (NAIT) has long been recognized for its strong programs in applied sciences, engineering, and technology. But when it came to specialized training in **Remotely Piloted Aircraft Systems (RPAS)**, NAIT looked for a partner with proven expertise, real-world experience, and a culturally grounded approach. That partner was **Aboriginal Training Services (ATS)**.
<br><br>
The collaboration was designed to give NAIT students something beyond textbooks and lectures. ATS brought its **hands-on training model**, blending Transport Canada‚Äìcompliant instruction with practical field missions and cultural awareness. This partnership ensured that students not only learned the theory of drone operations but also gained the confidence to fly, map, and interpret data in real-world contexts.
<br><br>
For NAIT, the partnership represented a chance to expand its academic offerings with industry-ready skills. For ATS, it was an opportunity to demonstrate how Indigenous-led training programs can thrive within a university setting ‚Äî bridging traditional stewardship values with the demands of modern higher education.

Aboriginal Training Services (ATS) stands apart because it does more than deliver technical content ‚Äî it **transforms how students engage with technology**. At NAIT, this meant bringing a teaching style designed for communities and adapting it seamlessly to a higher education environment.
<br><br>
ATS methods are built on three pillars. First, **Transport Canada compliance** ensures that every student is prepared to meet national licensing requirements, giving them credentials that are recognized across Canada. Second, **hands-on field training** moves learning beyond the classroom so students can plan missions, conduct flights, and produce usable outputs such as maps and models. Third, **cultural grounding** encourages students to see drones not only as technical tools but also as instruments of stewardship, responsibility, and connection to the land.
<br><br>
These methods proved to be a perfect complement to NAIT‚Äôs academic strengths. Where the university provided structure, research context, and resources, ATS brought in practical expertise, lived experience, and an approach that recognized the importance of sovereignty and cultural values.

A key strength of the NAIT‚ÄìATS partnership was the way it bridged **classroom learning with field application**. Students were not confined to theory; instead, they experienced the full cycle of professional RPAS operations.
<br><br>
In the classroom, ATS instructors guided students through regulations, airspace classifications, safety procedures, and mission planning. This built a solid foundation of knowledge and helped learners understand how Transport Canada rules apply in real scenarios.
<br><br>
Outdoors, those lessons came to life. Students launched drones in mapped flight grids, captured aerial imagery, and later processed that data into orthomosaics and 3D models. This combination of academic knowledge and applied skills gave learners a clear picture of how drones are used in environmental monitoring, engineering projects, and research.

ATS‚Äôs training model recognizes that drones are not just technical devices, but **tools of sovereignty and stewardship**. By flying over rivers, forests, and cultural sites, students were able to visualize territory in new ways while grounding that work in the teachings of their communities. Learners in GIS and data analysis integrated drone imagery into mapping workflows, turning raw imagery into actionable information.
<br><br>
For Indigenous students, these skills extended beyond the campus.
