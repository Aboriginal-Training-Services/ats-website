Across Canada, Indigenous communities are embracing drone technology as a practical tool for protecting lands and waters. With vast territories to monitor, from old-growth forests to salmon-bearing rivers, the challenge has always been speed, cost, and access to timely information. Aboriginal Training Services (ATS) bridges that gap by delivering specialised drone training programmes that blend Transport Canada certification standards with Indigenous knowledge and stewardship priorities.

To date, ATS has worked with more than **50 communities**, training **over 500 participants** in drone operations, mapping, and data analysis. These trainees have completed more than **1,000 supervised flights**, producing aerial imagery and monitoring data that covers large portions of Alberta and British Columbia. The results are tangible: communities now detect environmental changes faster, reduce dependence on costly external contractors, and strengthen sovereignty over how data is collected and used.

The success rate of these programmes is equally compelling. **Ninety-nine percent of students pass their Transport Canada examinations on the first attempt**, demonstrating both the quality of instruction and the dedication of participants. Graduates leave not only with certificates but also with the confidence to lead local monitoring efforts, apply drone skills to conservation and resource management, and inspire youth to see future careers in science and technology.

Indigenous Nations are responsible for stewarding some of the largest and most ecologically diverse territories in Canada. These lands and waters face mounting pressures: **illegal logging**, **wildfire risk**, **climate-driven flooding**, **riverbank erosion**, **industrial development**, and the need to **protect wildlife habitats**. Traditional monitoring methods â€” helicopter flights, consultant reports, or occasional government surveys â€” often arrive late, cost too much, or lack the depth of local knowledge required to understand change on the ground.

Communities increasingly require **real-time, locally controlled environmental intelligence** to safeguard forests, track water quality, assess post-fire regeneration, monitor cultural heritage sites, and respond to emerging threats. Drone technology provides a solution that is not only affordable and precise but also adaptable to seasonal cycles and community-driven priorities.

Aboriginal Training Services (ATS) builds programmes with one clear vision: to make Indigenous communities **keepers of the land, keepers of the data, and keepers of the environment**. Each training initiative aims to:

- **Build local drone pilot capacity** so monitoring is community-led.  
- **Support Indigenous data sovereignty**, ensuring data ownership stays with the Nation.  
- **Reduce dependency** on outside contractors and costly aerial services.  
- **Create career pathways** for youth and professionals in conservation, forestry, resource management, and technology.  
- **Blend cultural protocols** with cutting-edge aerospace practices so monitoring respects both ancestral knowledge and modern standards.  

By combining these objectives, ATS ensures that every flight not only produces high-quality imagery but also strengthens the autonomy and resilience of the communities it serves.

Before adopting drone technology, Indigenous communities faced serious challenges in monitoring their lands and waters. Environmental change often went undetected until it was too late to respond effectively. **Chartered helicopter and plane flights** were prohibitively expensive, sometimes costing tens of thousands of dollars for a single survey. Because flights could only be arranged a few times per year, important seasonal changes â€” such as flood events, wildlife migration, or forest disease outbreaks â€” were often missed.

Communities also relied heavily on **outside consultants and contractors** who would arrive, collect data, and leave without building local skills or returning meaningful knowledge. Reports were often delayed by months and rarely reflected cultural protocols or community priorities. This dependence limited self-determination and forced Nations to make decisions with incomplete or outdated information.

Even when data was available, **ownership and access remained a challenge**. Imagery and analysis were sometimes stored with third parties, creating barriers to long-term comparisons and raising questions of sovereignty. On-the-ground monitoring teams faced risks from harsh weather, wildfire smoke, remote terrain, and wildlife encounters, making fieldwork dangerous and difficult.

Without modern, accessible monitoring tools, Indigenous leaders were left with high costs, limited data, and few opportunities to create sustainable local employment in environmental management.

Aboriginal Training Services (ATS) responds to these challenges with a comprehensive training programme designed specifically for Indigenous communities. The programme combines **Transport Canadaâ€“aligned certification**, **hands-on flight practice**, and **community-centred monitoring applications**. Rather than importing expertise, ATS builds it from within, ensuring that every participant leaves with the confidence and credentials to lead environmental monitoring efforts at home.

The curriculum is modular and adaptable. Participants begin with the fundamentals of drone operations, including safety protocols, airspace awareness, flight planning, and emergency procedures. Training then expands into environmental applications: conducting aerial surveys of forests, mapping rivers and floodplains, assessing wildfire recovery zones, and documenting cultural heritage sites. By pairing modern aerospace knowledge with Indigenous stewardship values, ATS ensures that technology strengthens rather than replaces traditional ways of caring for the land.

Equally important, ATS prioritises **data sovereignty**. Trainees learn not only how to capture imagery but also how to manage, archive, and analyse it under community-defined protocols. This keeps control of sensitive environmental data in the hands of the people most affected by it. The result is a workforce that is both technically skilled and culturally grounded, able to operate drones safely and transform aerial data into actionable insights for leadership.

Every ATS training programme begins with **community engagement**. Leadership, guardians, and elders are consulted to identify monitoring priorities and culturally sensitive areas. This ensures the programme is not a one-size-fits-all package but instead tailored to the unique environmental and cultural context of each Nation.

From there, participants enter a structured training pathway. **Classroom sessions** introduce the fundamentals of drone operations, covering safety procedures, Transport Canada regulations, and mission planning. The programme then moves outdoors for **hands-on flight practicums**. Participants practise basic manoeuvres, emergency procedures, and controlled flights before progressing to real monitoring missions. Flights are carried out over rivers, forests, and wetlands identified by the community as priority sites. Under instructor supervision, trainees capture high-resolution imagery, set up ground control points, and learn how to process data into usable maps and reports.

The training culminates in **community-driven monitoring projects**. These projects allow participants to apply their skills to real-world concerns, whether it is assessing post-wildfire regeneration, mapping shoreline erosion, or tracking water quality. Results are shared back with leadership, reinforcing the value of local capacity and giving communities a new, practical tool for decision-making.

Drone monitoring works because it is **repeatable, precise, and adaptable to local needs**. ATS teaches participants to design flights that can be repeated season after season, ensuring consistent data for long-term comparisons. In forestry applications, drones capture detailed images of canopy health, storm damage, and regeneration after logging or wildfire. River systems are monitored through **linear flight paths**, allowing communities to assess erosion, sediment movement, and seasonal changes in water levels. Wetlands and cultural heritage sites are documented with **high-resolution orthomosaics** that can be layered into maps and shared in council meetings or planning sessions.

When needed, ATS introduces specialised sensors such as **thermal cameras** to detect seepage or hotspots, and **multispectral cameras** to measure vegetation health. These tools give communities deeper insights into ecosystem changes without the expense of hiring outside contractors.

For Indigenous communities, technology is only valuable when it strengthens sovereignty and aligns with cultural values. That principle guides every aspect of ATS training. Beyond flight skills and technical workflows, participants learn how to manage environmental data responsibly and in accordance with community-defined protocols.

**Data sovereignty** is central. All imagery and analysis generated during training belongs to the community and remains under their control. ATS emphasises secure storage on locally managed drives or servers, clear versioning systems, and transparent permissions for who can access or share results.

**Ethics in drone use** also play a major role. Trainees are taught to respect privacy, avoid flights over residential areas without consent, and carefully consider the cultural significance of the sites they are monitoring. Elders often help guide these decisions, ensuring that drone missions do not intrude on sacred spaces or undermine traditional practices.

ATS programmes reinforce the idea that Indigenous Nations are not only **keepers of the land** but also **keepers of the data**. Each training cohort leaves with not just technical knowledge but also an ethical framework for responsible, respectful use of technology in service of the environment and their people.

Safety is the foundation of every ATS training programme. Participants are introduced to Transport Canada regulations from the very first session and learn how to plan and conduct flights that meet national standards. This includes airspace awareness, weather assessment, pre-flight and post-flight checklists, and clear emergency procedures. Practical drills are built into every stage of the training. Trainees rehearse what to do in the event of a lost signal, sudden weather change, or low battery emergency. Compliance also means accountability: every flight is documented with logs that track location, duration, and outcomes.
